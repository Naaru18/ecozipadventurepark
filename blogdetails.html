The 30 Seconds Before You Launch

Discover the rituals, safety checks, and mindset that transform every zipline ride from a thrilling moment into a safe and unforgettable adventure.

Share

Most riders spend the hours before their session thinking about the descent. The speed. The scream they'll definitely not make but definitely will. What very few think about is the thirty seconds that determine whether any of that actually happens safely. That window is ours — the guide's — and it follows the same sequence every single time.

The Checklist Is Not a Formality

When a new guide joins the team, the first thing I tell them is this: the checklist is not there to satisfy a regulator. It is there because human beings have confirmation bias, and they have it worst when they're in a rush. The checklist is the thing that fights that.

We run twelve points across four categories — harness, connection hardware, rider posture, and clearance. Every point has a verbal call and a physical check. The rider hears it and sees it. That matters, because a rider who understands what's being verified is a rider who can flag something we might have missed.

"The checklist is not there to satisfy a regulator. It's there because human beings have confirmation bias, and they have it worst when they're in a rush."

Point by Point: What We're Actually Looking For

The harness check starts at the chest strap and works outward. We're not just confirming it's buckled — we're confirming it's threaded correctly, which is a different and much harder thing to verify at speed. A buckled-but-wrong harness will hold your weight right up until it doesn't.

Connection hardware is where most incidents in our industry trace back to. Not to faulty equipment — to correctly-rated equipment that was connected in a hurry. We use a double-lock carabiner on every line, and the verification isn't visual: it's tactile. Pull, twist, pull. If it opens, something is wrong.

Rider Posture: The One Thing Riders Can Control

Everything up to this point has been our responsibility. Posture is theirs, with coaching from us. We're looking for three things:

  • Chin up and eyes forward — riders who look down shift their centre of gravity unpredictably
  • Arms at rest, not gripping the trolley — a death-grip means tension, and tension means a harder landing
  • Knees soft, never locked — the same principle as any impact absorption

Most first-timers arrive at the platform with locked knees and white knuckles. We have about twenty seconds to talk them out of that. The ones who can hear us — really hear us — are the ones who land clean.

Clearance and the Final Call

The final step belongs to the receiving platform, not the launch guide. We radio ahead. We get a confirmed clear. No exceptions, no assumptions about timing. A slow rider on the previous run, a bird on the line, a maintenance crew who overran their window — these are real things that have happened. The radio call is the reason they didn't become incidents.

Only after we have a verbal clear from the far end do we give the count. Three. Two. One. And then it's physics, and everything we did in those thirty seconds is either holding or it isn't.

"The ones who can really hear us are the ones who land clean."

What This Feels Like From the Rider's Side

We've had riders tell us the check felt slow, or fussy, or like we were delaying for its own sake. We've also had riders — usually ones who've been on other lines at other parks — tell us they could feel the difference. That it felt serious in a way that made them feel looked after rather than processed.

That's the goal. The thirty seconds are not a delay. They are the ride. Everything after is just the part you'll tell people about.
